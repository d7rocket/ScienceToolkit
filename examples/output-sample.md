## Slide 3: A Telescope Built to See Back in Time

Webb observes in infrared, letting it pierce dust clouds and detect light stretched by cosmic expansion over 13 billion years. Its 6.5-meter mirror captures detail no previous telescope could reach. But the oldest galaxies are its most stunning challenge yet.

## Slide 4: What the Light Tells Us

The spectral signatures locked in each galaxy's light reveal its age, chemical makeup, and distance. Webb's NIRCam instrument separated light from a crowded field and pinpointed individual galaxy candidates. Each photon is a message from the early universe — scientists are still decoding it.

## Caption

What do you do when the universe doesn't follow the rules? You rewrite the textbook — and that's exactly what the James Webb Space Telescope is forcing astronomers to do.

JWST launched in December 2021 and reached its final orbit around the L2 Lagrange point six months later. When scientists turned it toward deep space and released the first full-colour images in July 2022, jaws dropped. Not just because of the beauty — but because the data was already breaking models.

Among the most startling findings: two galaxy candidates at redshifts of approximately z = 10 to 12. That places them just 300 to 400 million years after the Big Bang. These aren't faint smudges — they're surprisingly luminous, suggesting enormous stellar mass accumulated at a pace that current simulations simply can't explain.

Here's why that matters. Galaxy formation models predict that it takes time — hundreds of millions to billions of years — for gravity to pull gas and dark matter together into the dense halos where stars ignite. Finding galaxies this massive this early is like discovering a skyscraper in a city that was farmland last year. Something isn't adding up.

One possibility: dark matter halos in the early universe collapsed faster than models assume, giving gravity a head start. Another: the initial mass function of stars — the mix of large to small stars born in each burst of formation — was tilted toward massive, luminous stars in the early universe, producing more light per unit of mass. A third option, more speculative: there are gaps in our understanding of fundamental cosmology.

Spectroscopic confirmation of these candidates is ongoing. Some redshift estimates based on photometric data alone have been revised as follow-up observations come in. Science at the frontier is messy — but that's where the discoveries live.

JWST is just getting started. Its instruments are rated for a 10-year minimum mission, with fuel reserves for potentially 20 years. Every month brings new data, new puzzles, and new chances to revise what we thought we knew.
