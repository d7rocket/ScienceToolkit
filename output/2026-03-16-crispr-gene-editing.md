## Caption

What happens when CRISPR gene editing is used not to fix human DNA, but to strip drug resistance right out of bacteria?

Drug-resistant bugs are one of the top health risks on Earth. They kill more than 1.2 million people each year, and by 2050 that toll may hit 10 million. How do they spread so fast? Bacteria pass resistance genes to one another on tiny DNA rings through a process called conjugation. One cell can arm a whole colony in hours. These genes even hop between species — a harmless soil bug can gift drug defenses to a lethal one. We tried to keep up by making new drugs, but no major new class has come to market in decades. Most of what we do now is try to slow the spread: cut overuse, clean hospitals, watch farms. But none of these steps strip resistance away once it takes hold, and the clock is ticking.

A study in *npj Antimicrobials and Resistance* describes a tool that does just that. A UC San Diego team built pPro-MobV, a CRISPR system based on gene drives — the same trick used in insects to block malaria. It rides the channels bacteria use to share resistance, but deletes resistance genes instead. The bugs disarm themselves. And it works inside biofilms, the dense bacterial mats that drugs can't reach.

That last part is key. Biofilms coat hospital gear, wounds, and pipes. They act as shields that block drugs and cleaning agents. Most tools fail here. But pPro-MobV slips in through the cell links bacteria build inside these mats, turning their best shield into a way in. As lead researcher Justin Meyer put it, this is one of the only tools that actively reverses resistance — not just slows it. The team also showed it pairs with phage, viruses that prey on bacteria, hinting at combo treatments for hospitals, farms, and water systems. If it scales from the lab, it could bring back drugs we thought we had lost.

If bugs can evolve to beat our drugs, can we build tools that outpace evolution itself?
